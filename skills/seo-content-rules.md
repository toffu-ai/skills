Apply these rules whenever writing or reviewing content intended to rank in organic search.

## What Google Actually Ranks

- Google ranks pages that satisfy search intent better than competitors, not pages that follow SEO checklists. A 600-word page that directly answers the query will outrank a 3,000-word guide that buries the answer.
- Topical authority matters more than individual page optimization. One article on a topic won't rank against a site with 30 articles in the same cluster. Build content clusters, not isolated pages.
- E-E-A-T (Experience, Expertise, Authority, Trust) is not a ranking factor you can optimize with a checklist. It's the cumulative effect of author credentials, original data, cited sources, and real-world experience visible in the content. "Written by a marketing team" is invisible. "Written by Sarah Chen, who managed $50M in ad spend" is not.

## Content That Actually Competes

- Before writing anything, look at what already ranks. If the top 10 results are all product comparison pages, don't write a how-to guide. Match the format and intent, then be better.
- "Better" means: more specific examples, more recent data, better structure, original research or screenshots, and a perspective. Saying the same thing as everyone else with more words is not a content strategy.
- Programmatic pages work when you have proprietary data or a unique angle at scale (e.g., "Google Ads benchmarks for [industry]" x 50 industries). They fail when they're thin templates with swapped keywords. Google will index and then deindex thin programmatic content within months.
- Internal linking is not a checklist item. It's information architecture. Every piece of content should link to 3-5 related pages using descriptive anchor text. Link from high-authority pages to pages you want to rank. Don't link everything to your homepage.

## Technical Truths

- Core Web Vitals are a tiebreaker, not a primary ranking factor. Don't spend weeks optimizing page speed on a page with weak content. But also don't ship pages that take 8 seconds to load.
- Schema markup gets you rich snippets, not higher rankings. It's worth implementing for FAQ, how-to, and product pages because rich snippets increase CTR from the SERP. It won't move you from position 15 to position 3.
- Canonical tags, hreflang, and sitemap hygiene prevent problems. They don't create wins. Fix them when they're broken, don't obsess over them when they're fine.
- Title tags and meta descriptions are CTR optimization tools, not ranking factors (beyond keyword relevance). Write them for humans looking at a search results page, not for crawlers.

## What Wastes Time

- Optimizing for keywords with zero search volume "for long-term growth." Traffic follows search volume. Write for queries people actually search.
- Publishing on a schedule for its own sake. One excellent piece per month outranks four mediocre pieces per week. Google measures quality signals per page, not publishing cadence.
- Rewriting old content that never ranked. If it didn't rank after 6 months with no traction, the topic or angle is wrong. Write a new piece with a different approach rather than polishing a failure.
- Chasing algorithm updates reactively. Most "updates" don't affect well-built sites. If your traffic dropped after an update, the issue predated the update - Google just caught up.
